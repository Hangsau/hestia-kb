## Abstract

This study examined whether jerk cost (JC) and resultant mechanical impulse can discriminate swimming performance in young swimmers. Sixty-nine young swimmers (37 girls, 32 boys; age 11–17) performed a 100m all-out front crawl trial. Jerk cost was computed as the ratio between the time derivative of resultant force and the resultant force magnitude, integrated over the propulsive phase. Results indicated that JC was significantly lower in faster swimmers (p < 0.01), and showed moderate-to-large effect sizes when discriminating performance groups (d = 0.71–0.89). Mechanical impulse was also significantly different between performance groups (p < 0.001). The authors conclude that JC is a sensitive discriminator of swimming performance in youth swimmers and may serve as a useful biomechanical feedback tool for coaches.

## Technical Relevance

- **Jerk cost** = smoothness of force application; lower = smoother, more efficient pull
- Applies to: freestyle pull phase (high relevance), all strokes (supporting evidence)
- Practical use: coaches can use underwater push-off force plates to calculate jerk cost as technical efficiency metric

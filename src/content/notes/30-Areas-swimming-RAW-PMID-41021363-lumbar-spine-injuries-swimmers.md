## Abstract

Lumbar spine injuries are a frequent overuse injury among competitive swimmers, yet prevention strategies have not been well studied. This retrospective study evaluated 285 swimmers (mean age 18.7 ± 3.1 years) to identify risk factors. Demographics, stroke specialties, training characteristics, and injury history were collected. Group comparisons were conducted with t-tests and chi-square analyses, and logistic regression was used to calculate odds ratios (OR) with 95% confidence intervals (CI). Twenty-nine swimmers (10.2%) reported lumbar spine injuries, including spondylolysis, lumbar strain, disc pathology, and facet joint pain. Injured athletes were older, trained more hours in the pool, competed at a higher level, were hypermobile, and used kickboards less often. Adjusted regression identified older age, higher competition level, and hypermobility as independent risk factors for lumbar spine injury.

## Technical Relevance to Stroke Mechanics

- **Freestyle/Butterfly**: Higher lumbar injury rates associated with extended hyperextension during pull-through and breath phases
- **IM specialists**: Higher cumulative lumbar load across all four strokes
- **Kickboard usage**: Protective factor (reduced pool hours = less lumbar stress)

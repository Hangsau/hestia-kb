# PMID-20166004 — Does breathing disturb coordination in butterfly?

**Seifert L, Chollet D, Sanders R** — Int J Sports Med 2010 Mar;31(3):167-173

## Abstract

This study quantified the effects of breathing compared to non-breathing and "race pace" on arm-to-leg coordination in the butterfly stroke. Twelve elite male swimmers swam at four paces: 400m, 200m, 100m, and 50m.

**Methods:** Arm and leg stroke phases were identified by video analysis to calculate the total time gap (TTG), the sum of T1 (hands' entry/high point of first kick), T2 (beginning of hands' backward movement/low point of first kick), T3 (hands' arrival vertical to shoulders/high point of second kick), and T4 (hands' release from water/low point of second kick).

**Key findings:**
- TTG was **greater with breathing (23.3%)** vs. without breathing (19%) (p < 0.05)
- Breathing causes **less propulsive continuity** between arm and leg actions
- Breathing caused: **shorter downward leg kick** + **longer arm catch** + **longer upward leg kick** + **longer glide time**
- Breathing leads to **greater coupling** between hand exit and end of leg propulsion (shorter arm push phase to facilitate head exit to breathe)

**Interpretation for the document:**
- The "head exit to breathe" requirement forces the arm push phase to shorten = one stroke cycle's propulsion loss
- The coordination change with breathing is systematic, not random — it has a consistent direction
- The "shorter downward kick" is significant: the kick that initiates the wave is reduced when breathing
- The breathing disruption to coordination is proportional to the breathing frequency used

**Keywords:** butterfly, breathing, coordination, arm-leg, total time gap, elite swimmers

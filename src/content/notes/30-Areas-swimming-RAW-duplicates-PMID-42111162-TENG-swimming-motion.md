## Abstract

This study developed a single-wire structured triboelectric nanogenerator (TENG) for self-powered swimming motion monitoring. The device converts mechanical energy from limb movements into electrical signals without external power. Twelve competitive swimmers performed standardized stroke trials while wearing the TENG sensor at the wrist and ankle. The system successfully captured stroke frequency, kick rate, and arm stroke cycle timing with >92% accuracy compared to video analysis. Real-time feedback was provided via a wireless receiver. The authors demonstrate that the TENG-based system offers a practical, low-cost solution for in-water biomechanical monitoring during training.

## Technical Relevance

- **Wearable motion sensors**: Objective measurement of stroke frequency, kick rate, and stroke cycle timing
- Supports technique monitoring: Real-time feedback on rhythm deviations from optimal patterns
- Relevance: Potential coach-facing tool for tracking technique consistency across training sessions

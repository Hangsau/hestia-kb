# PMID-35935061 — Improving tumble turn performance in swimming: the impact of wall contact time and tuck index

**David S, Grove T, Duijven MV, Koster P, Beek PJ** — Front Sports Act Living 2022 Jul 22;4:936695

## Abstract

Race time can be shortened by improving turn performance. The aim was to examine the effect of Wall Contact Time (WCT) and Tuck Index on tumble turn performance and their interrelations. Eighteen Dutch national level swimmers (FINA points 552 ± 122) performed tumble turns with three different WCTs (shorter, preferred, longer) and three different Tuck Indices (higher, preferred, lower), recorded by four underwater cameras and a wall-mounted force plate.

**Key quantified findings:**
- WCT must be **sufficiently long** to generate a high push-off force at the end of wall contact when the body is in a streamlined position
- Shorter WCT = significantly **longer 5m round-trip time** (worse performance)
- Linear mixed effect model: negative significant effects of WCT (-4.22, p < 0.001), FPeak (-2.18, p = 0.04), Vin (-4.83, p = 0.02), Tadapt (-2.68, p = 0.002), and Vexit (-9.52, p < 0.001) on 5mRTT
- **Optimal Tuck Index = 0.7** (some swimmers could benefit from adapting their distance to the wall)
- The relationship between WCT and performance is non-linear: too short is worse, but "preferred" is optimal

**Interpretation for the document:**
- WCT is a **controlled variable**, not just an outcome — swimmers can modulate it intentionally
- The key insight: WCT must be long enough for the body to reach streamline AND for the neuromuscular system to generate peak force in a compact position
- Tuck Index 0.7 = a specific geometric relationship (tuck depth relative to reach); individual calibration is possible
- "Shorter is not always better" for WCT — this contradicts common coaching intuition

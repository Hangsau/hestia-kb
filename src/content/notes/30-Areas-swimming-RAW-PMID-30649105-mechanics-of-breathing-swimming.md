# PMID-30649105 — The Mechanics of Breathing during Swimming

**Leahy MG, Summers MN, Peters CM, Molgat-Seon Y, Geary CM, Sheel AW** — Med Sci Sports Exerc 2019 Jul;51(7):1467-1476

## Abstract

The thorax undergoes unique conditions while swimming. Hydrostatic pressure from water immersion places an external load on the thorax and increases airway resistance, and the horizontal body position results in central venous engorgement and an associated reduction in lung compliance.

**Methods:** Eight collegiate swimmers performed an incremental swim test while tethered to a resistance apparatus, and an incremental cycle test on a separate day. Inspired work of breathing (Wb) was quantified using an esophageal balloon catheter.

**Key findings:**
- Peak VO2 similar between swimming and cycling (3.87 ± 0.92 vs 4.20 ± 0.83 L·min)
- Peak minute ventilation (V̇E) significantly **lower** during swimming (118 ± 3 vs 154 ± 25 L·min)
- Heart rate significantly **lower** during swimming (164 ± 19 vs 183 ± 8 bpm)
- Inspired Wb was **significantly higher** at V̇E of 50, 75, and 100 L·min during swimming (e.g., +56 ± 23 J·min at 75 L·min)
- Periods of **interbreath apnea** observed while swimming (duration = 0.13–2.07 s)

**Interpretation for the document:**
- Water immersion imposes a unique "external load" on the thorax that makes each breath mechanically more expensive than equivalent land exercise
- Interbreath apnea episodes (0.13–2.07 s) explain why swimming feels "breath-starved" even with adequate oxygen — the breathing mechanics are the bottleneck, not cardiovascular capacity
- Lower HR during swimming vs cycling at similar VO2 = swimming is limited by ventilatory mechanics before cardiovascular mechanics
- This is the physiological underpinning for why breathing efficiency is a swim-specific skill, not just a fitness factor

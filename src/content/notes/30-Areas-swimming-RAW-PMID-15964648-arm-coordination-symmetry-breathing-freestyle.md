# PMID-15964648 — Arm coordination symmetry and breathing effect in front crawl

**Seifert L, Chollet D, Allard P** — Hum Mov Sci 2005 Apr;24(2):234-256

## Abstract

This study analysed the relationships among arm coordination symmetry, motor laterality and breathing laterality during a 100-m front crawl, as a function of expertise. Ten elite swimmers (G1), 10 mid-level swimmers (G2), and 8 non-expert swimmers (G3) composed three skill groups, distinguished by velocity, stroke rate, stroke length, breathing frequency (BF) and the mean number of strokes between two breaths (stroke breath, SB) over a 100-m front crawl.

Four stroke phases were identified by video analysis (catch, pull, push and recovery) and the index of coordination (IdC) measured the lag time between the propulsive phases of the two arms. Three modes of coordination: catch-up (IdC<0%), opposition (IdC=0%) and superposition (IdC>0%).

Most front crawl swimmers showed asymmetric arm coordination, with propulsive discontinuity on one side and propulsive superposition on the other. This asymmetry was most often related to breathing laterality.

**Interpretation for the document:**
- Breathing laterality → arm coordination asymmetry → both are linked and systematic
- Elite swimmers manage the asymmetry to maintain efficiency on both sides
- "Bilateral breathing" as a training tool makes sense: training both breathing sides reduces the asymmetry gap
- Motor laterality (handedness) and breathing laterality interact to shape the stroke pattern

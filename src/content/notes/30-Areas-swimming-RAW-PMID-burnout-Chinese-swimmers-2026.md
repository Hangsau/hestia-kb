## Abstract

Athlete burnout significantly affects both athlete well-being and performance, potentially influenced by dietary patterns, sleep quality, screen time, and stress-coping strategies. However, the mechanistic interplay among these factors in competitive swimmers remains unclear. This cross-sectional study investigated relationships between diet quality, sleep patterns, screen time, stress coping strategies, psychological strain, and burnout in Chinese competitive swimmers. Two hundred and forty-seven competitive swimmers (mean age 15.8 ± 2.3 years) completed validated questionnaires assessing all variables. Hierarchical regression analysis revealed that poor sleep quality (β = 0.34, p < 0.001), high screen time (β = 0.21, p < 0.01), maladaptive stress coping (β = 0.28, p < 0.001), and lower diet quality (β = −0.19, p < 0.01) independently predicted burnout. The authors conclude that multimodal interventions addressing sleep hygiene, screen time, coping strategies, and nutrition may reduce burnout risk in competitive swimmers.

## Technical Relevance

- **Sleep quality → technical performance**: Sleep deprivation impairs motor learning and technique consolidation — relevant to technical skill acquisition
- **Burnout → training consistency**: Swimmers experiencing burnout show reduced training quality, indirectly affecting technique maintenance
- **Indirect relationship**: This study does not address stroke-specific biomechanics but informs training program design that supports consistent technique development

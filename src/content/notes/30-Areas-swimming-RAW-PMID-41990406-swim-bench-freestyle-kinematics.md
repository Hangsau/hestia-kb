## Abstract

The swim bench is an isokinetic ergometer designed to replicate the underwater pull of the freestyle stroke for swimming training. Limited literature addresses the biomechanical fidelity of the swim bench and specific changes to a swimmer's kinematics with the addition of a torso rotation setting. This study assessed the influence of swim bench rotation settings on freestyle stroke kinematics and evaluated differences with pre-existing in-water kinematic data.

Methods: Both rotating and fixed swim bench settings compared to in-water freestyle kinematics.

Key findings:
- Few kinematic differences noted between swim bench settings
- Both swim bench settings produced: shorter entry, longer pull/push/recovery stroke cycle phase durations (p < 0.0001), shorter stroke length (p = 0.0002), narrower mean peak stroke width (p < 0.0001)
- Restricted total shoulder roll: rotating 37° ± 28, fixed 33° ± 26, in-water 96° ± 7.0 (p < 0.0001)
- In-water shoulder roll is 2.5x greater than even the rotating swim bench setting

Conclusion: The addition of a rotating setting to the swim bench may not substantially supplement simulation of the underwater freestyle pull. The movement performed on the swim bench may not be sufficiently sport specific to allow training and research findings to be extrapolated to the pool.

## Technical Relevance

- Swim bench training may be limited by structural differences vs actual swimming
- 96° shoulder roll in-water vs 37° on swim bench — massive discrepancy
- This is important for dryland training: swim bench pull is NOT equivalent to in-water pull
- Key implication for coaching: don't over-rely on swim bench for pull technique training

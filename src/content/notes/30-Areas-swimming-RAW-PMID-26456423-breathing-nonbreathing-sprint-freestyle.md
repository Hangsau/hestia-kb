# PMID-26456423 — Upper limb kinematic differences between breathing and non-breathing conditions in front crawl sprint swimming

**McCabe CB, Sanders RH, Psycharakis SG** — J Biomech 2015 Nov 26;48(15):3995-4001

## Abstract

The purpose of this study was to determine whether the breathing action in front crawl (FC) sprint swimming affects the ipsilateral upper limb kinematics relative to a non-breathing stroke cycle (SC). Ten male competitive swimmers performed two 25m FC sprints: one breathing to their preferred side (Br) and one not breathing (NBr).

**Key findings (quantified performance cost of breathing):**
- Swimmers were **3% slower** when breathing
- Entry phase: slower COM horizontal velocity (3.3%), less shoulder flexion (8%), abduction (33%), and roll (4%) when breathing
- Pull phase: **longer in duration (14%)**, shallower hand path (11%), less shoulder abduction (11%), slower hand vertical acceleration (30%), slower COM horizontal velocity (3%)
- Push phase: smaller elbow ROM (38%), faster backwards hand speed (25%), faster hand vertical acceleration (33%)
- Recovery phase: swimmers rolled their shoulders more (12%) when breathing

**Practical recommendations from authors:**
1. "Swimmers aim to orient their ipsilateral shoulder into a stronger position by **stretching and rolling the shoulders more in the entry phase** whilst preparing to take a breath"
2. "Focus on **lengthening the push phase by extending the elbow more** and not accelerating the hand too quickly upwards when preparing to inhale"

**Interpretation for the document:**
- 3% speed cost is the sprint-specific finding; distance swimmers may have lower costs
- The breathing-side shoulder must actively prepare for the roll BEFORE the breath, not during it
- The push phase shortening on the breathing side = less propulsive time = one mechanism of speed loss
- Roll deficit is 4% on entry → this is the "stretching preparation" cue in the entry phase

**Keywords:** front crawl, breathing, sprint, kinematics, shoulder, performance

## Abstract

Systematic scoping review synthesizing physiological and biomechanical indicators applied in swimmers, focusing on acute and long-term responses, reliability and validity of these indicators. Web of Science, PubMed, and SPORTDiscus searched; study screening conducted using ASReview. 41 studies included; 297 evaluation indicators extracted. Indicators classified according to conceptual definitions and quantitative criteria (ICC, CV for reliability; Pearson correlation and R² for validity).

Key findings:
- Existing research predominantly concentrated on highly trained adult male swimmers
- Limited discussion given to elite/world-class athletes, adolescents, and female swimmers
- Acute testing dominates the literature (83.5%), far outweighing long-term monitoring approaches
- Biomechanical measures account for majority (59%), kinematic variables most frequently applied (61%)
- Only ~8% of acute indicators and ~3% of long-term indicators simultaneously achieved at least moderate reliability AND validity
- Key determinants of aquatic performance — specifically propulsive kinetics and metabolic cost — LACK confirmed reliability for tracking adaptations across training macrocycles

Conclusion: The field requires a shift from proliferation of novel swim indices to rigorous methodological consolidation of existing aquatic assessment protocols. Propulsive kinetics and metabolic cost cannot currently be reliably tracked across macrocycles.

## Technical Relevance

- This is a meta-level finding: the field has too many indices, not enough validation
- Propulsive kinetics and metabolic cost are the two most important performance determinants — but they CANNOT be reliably tracked over seasons
- 83.5% of studies are acute tests, not longitudinal monitoring — huge gap in longitudinal understanding
- The 8%/3% reliability-validity rates mean almost ALL proposed swim metrics are unproven for real training use
- For coaches: be skeptical of novel swim indices; focus on established measures with ICC > 0.75
